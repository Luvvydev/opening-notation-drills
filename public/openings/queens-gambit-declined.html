Queen's Gambit Declined

The Queen’s Gambit Declined keeps the center intact and leads to structures that have been studied for a long time. Black does not accept the c4 pawn and focuses on stable development.

  • Classical structure
  • Theory: Medium
  • Free

How the opening starts

The usual sequence is 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6. Black supports d5 and aims to develop without weakening the center. Many QGD positions are slow enough that piece placement and timing matter more than forcing tactics.

Move order preview
1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7

What matters early

  • Black is trying to complete development while staying ready for ...c5 or ...e5 later.
  • The c8 bishop needs a route. That route changes by variation.
  • Minor piece placement often decides whether Black is just equal or slightly cramped.

What usually goes wrong

  • Automatic development can leave the bishop on c8 buried for too long.
  • White often gets a small initiative if Black is careless with move order.
  • Do not underestimate exchange structure choices. They affect the whole middlegame.

Queen's Gambit Accepted

The Queen’s Gambit Accepted takes the c4 pawn and asks White to prove compensation through development and central pressure. Black tries to return the pawn under decent conditions or hold it briefly while finishing development.

King's Indian Defense

The King’s Indian Defense lets White build a center, then tries to attack that center with piece pressure and pawn breaks. Black often castles quickly and plays for ...e5 or ...c5, depending on the setup.
